The deep squat is one of the most technical — and most poorly executed — movements in the gym. Not because it is inherently dangerous, but because most people learn it incompletely and without quality feedback.

Over years of working with clients, I've identified a consistent pattern: the same seven mistakes appear again and again, regardless of experience level.

## Why technique matters more than you think

Before diving into the mistakes, let's clarify something: the deep squat with good technique is extraordinarily safe and effective for lower body muscle development. The scientific literature is consistent on this. Problems arise when range of motion is forced without the necessary mobility, or when load is added before the movement pattern is consolidated.

A study by Hartmann et al. (2013) in the *Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research* showed that compressive forces on the knee during a well-executed deep squat are manageable and not greater than those of a poorly executed partial squat.

## The 7 most common mistakes

### 1. Heels rising off the floor

The most visible mistake and the one that gives the most information. If your heels rise as you descend, there are three possible causes:

- Insufficient ankle mobility (most common)
- Long femurs relative to torso
- Incorrect foot position for your anatomy

**Solution:** Work ankle mobility with specific exercises (soleus stretches, band mobilizations) before each session. Experiment with foot rotation between 15 and 30 degrees. If the problem persists, using a small heel raise (or weightlifting shoes) is a pragmatic solution, not a shortcut.

### 2. Knee valgus collapse

Knees fall inward, especially at the most difficult point (in the hole). This can indicate:

- Weakness in hip abductors and gluteus medius
- Lack of conscious activation
- Excessive load for current technical level

**Solution:** Reduce the load until you can maintain knees aligned with feet throughout the range of motion. Add specific glute medius work: clamshells, monster walks, machine abduction. Conscious cuing ("push your knees out") can be sufficient in many cases.

### 3. Excessive forward torso lean

Some degree of torso inclination is unavoidable and normal in the squat. The problem arises when this angle is excessive and compromises bar position.

- Long femurs require more inclination; there is no anatomical alternative
- Limited hip mobility forces compensation through the torso
- Upper back weakness allows the bar to "win" against the lifter

**Solution:** Work hip mobility and hamstring flexibility. Consider goblet squat or front squat as corrective tools. If the issue is upper back weakness, strengthen it with rows, pull-downs, and specific work.

### 4. Pronounced butt wink at the bottom

"Butt wink" is the posterior pelvic tilt that occurs at the bottom of the squat. A small amount is normal and not problematic. The issue arises when it is pronounced and occurs well before parallel.

**Solution:** The correct approach is to work hip mobility and core activity, not simply limit depth artificially. Foot position also influences this: experiment with wider stance.

### 5. Incorrect breath management

Most lifters don't use intra-abdominal pressure correctly. The Valsalva mechanism (deep breath, diaphragm lock before descent) is fundamental for protecting the spine under load.

**Solution:** Learn and practice the Valsalva mechanism with light loads. The sequence: deep breath, lock, descend, ascend to 3/4 of the range, exhale.

### 6. Uncontrolled descent

A descent without control (falling into the hole) eliminates eccentric work, which is an important part of the hypertrophy stimulus, and makes the movement unpredictable.

**Solution:** Work on tempo. A controlled 2-3 second descent improves postural awareness, movement control, and hypertrophy stimulus.

### 7. Inconsistent depth between sets

If in your first set you reach below parallel and in your fifth you only reach parallel, you are not accumulating the training volume you think you are.

**Solution:** Use depth markers (a box or pin in the rack) to train to an objective standard. A side mirror is your best real-time feedback tool.

## The correct path

Correcting technique requires two things most people avoid: reducing ego (i.e., load) temporarily and being consistent with mobility work. There are no shortcuts.

Start with bodyweight or an empty bar, film your reps from the side, and identify your main error. Work on that error for 4-6 weeks before adding significant load.

The well-executed deep squat is a long-term investment in your ability to continue training hard and injury-free for years.
